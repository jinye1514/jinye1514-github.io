On-orbit Manipulation

Northrop Grumman Corporation

May 2024 - Current

Project Background

Every year, hundreds to thousands of satellites are launched into space. However, once these satellites are launched, they have limited fuel in their attitude control systems and they become effectively defunct when its fuel runs out, losing any orbital control capabilities.

In response to this challenge, Northrop Grumman (NG) has developed the Mission Extension Pod (MEP), a 'piggyback' satellite that can dock with a defunct satellite, and provide orbital control capabilities to extend the satellite's operational life. The MEP docks with the client satellite by inserting a probe into the Liquid Apogee Engine (LAE) nozzle of the client satellite. Along with the Mission Robotic Vehicle (MRV), which is equipped with a 7-DOF robotic arm, NG has shown initial success in capturing relatively stationary client satellites.

Carnegie Mellon University's Biorobotics Lab is collaborating with NG to develop the MRV's control system to further improve their capabilities to capture tumbling client satellites. Specifically, as the client satellite tumbles, contact becomes unavoidable between the probe and the nozzle. Because contact dynamics are highly nonlinear, and difficult to model, our team has developed a hardware emulator named Holodeck to generate real contact data to be used for evaluating the control system of the MRV in a simulated space environment.

Personal Contributions

Over the summer of 2024, I have been working extensively on the Holodeck project. I have been a key contributor to our custom ROS interface for synchronous control of the 28 DOF of the Holodeck emulator, and I have spearheaded the development of the emulator software to convert insertion simulation motion to real hardware motion of the Holodeck. In addition, I have created demonstrations of the Holodeck for the NG team, and for CMU faculty and students.

holodeck hardware

The Holodeck is a hardware-in-the-loop test platform that can emulate the dynamics of the MEP and the client satellite in a simulated space environment. The Holodeck is equipped with 4 UR10e robotic arms, each mounted on a Vention linear rail system, allowing 7 degrees of freedom per arm/rail combination.

holodeck control interface

The Holodeck control interface is a custom ROS package that allows for synchronous control of the total 28 DOF of the Holodeck emulator. The interface is designed to take high level commands from the user, the MRV-Client simulator in this case, and convert them to low level commands for the Holodeck hardware. The specific commands depend on the control mode, specified by the user.

Both the Arm and Carriage nodes are organized as state machines, with each state corresponding to a specific control mode. The state machines are designed to be modular, allowing for easy addition of new control modes. The state machines are also designed to be robust, with error handling and recovery mechanisms in place.

space gravity demo

The video above shows the Holodeck in action, emulating the dynamics of a ball in two simulated environments: space and water. During the first half of the video, the ball is in space without any gravity. When the ball is disturbed by an external force, the force torque sensor on the end effector of the arm detects the disturbance and passes the contact data to the simulation, which responds by moving the ball accordingly. Similarly, during the second half of the video, the ball is in water, but the gravity is set to be the same as on Earth. The ball, having a simulated density of 0.9 g/cm³, floats on a simulated water surface and can be pushed down, or lifted up above the water surface by external forces. The ball positions are passed from simulation and converted into end effector poses and passed to the Holodeck Control Interface to be converted into motion.

mrv-client docking demo

The following videos show the Holodeck emulating the MRV-Client docking process. The simulator contains a simulated space environment, with the MRV, MEP, and client satellite models. During each step of the simulation, the simulator node publishes the resultant motion of the probe and nozzle poses through ROS in real time. The poses are then converted into the corresponding joint velocities for each arm of the Holodeck using resolved rate control and passed to the Holodeck Control Interface to be converted into motion.

For this demo, the client satellite is experiencing a non-zero initial angular velocity and can experience contact with the probe during insertion.

EigenBot

Aug 2023 - Current

Project Background

The EigenBot is a modular hexapod using a novel bio-inspired distributed neural control architecture. In centralized control schemes, a central computer is responsible for all processes such as receiving user input, receiving sensor feedback, computing proceeding actions, etc. Instead, the EigenBot uses a distributed control architecture, where each leg of the robot is controlled by a separate neural network, handling its own sensor feedback and computing its own actions, while also receiving descending commands from its upstream networks. This control architecture, modeled after the nervous system of the Carausius morosus stick insect, allows for the robot to be able to adapt to changes in its environment, such as changing terrain, and even leg amputations, while being less computationally expensive than centralized control schemes.

Personal Contributions

I have formulated and implemented various compliance control algorithms for the EigenBot. The compliance control algorithms are modular to each joint, and are designed to allow the EigenBot to adapt to changes in its environment, such as uneven terrain. I tested the compliance control algorithms in simulation and on the real hardware, and have demonstrated enhanced locomotion performance on uneven terrain.
